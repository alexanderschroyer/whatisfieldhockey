## What you’re watching

Two teams try to score by hitting the ball into the opponent’s goal with the **flat side** of the stick. Play is continuous, tactical, and often fast on modern turf (water-based or sand-dressed, depending on venue).

## In the United States

Sanctioned outdoor play in the US typically tracks the **FIH Rules of Field Hockey** plus **USA Field Hockey** outdoor regulations. **NCAA** and **NFHS** (high school) publish their own **modifications** for their games. Current PDFs and briefings are linked from the [USA Field Hockey Rules of Field Hockey](https://www.usafieldhockey.com/rules-of-hockey) hub — use the rulebook for the competition you are watching.

**College (NCAA) orientation:** NCAA women’s field hockey follows the international rulebook with published modifications. One visible difference for fans: when a defender **unintentionally** sends the ball over the back line, the restart is often taken on the **25-yard / 23 m line** (in line with where the ball crossed), not at the classic **sideline long-corner** spot you may still see painted on some fields. **Overtime, shootouts, and card timing** can also differ from other FIH-based events — confirm with **NCAA** materials linked from that hub or the NCAA’s own publications. The sidebar links a plain-language [NCAA.com overview](https://www.ncaa.com/news/fieldhockey/article/2024-08-08/college-field-hockey-rules-explained) for fans (not a substitute for the official modification booklet).

**High school (NFHS):** Varsity and other sanctioned **high school** games in the US follow **NFHS** field hockey rules (with state association application). That is **different** from the international rulebook fans see on TV and from college modifications. Use the **NFHS** rulebook for the competition you are watching, not this site’s summary.

**Youth, PE, and community programs (FUNdamental Field Hockey):** The filename **FUNFH** refers to USA Field Hockey’s **FUNdamental Field Hockey Guide** (PDF in the sidebar) — a **programming and teaching** resource for schools, camps, recreation departments, and youth organizations. It is **not** the NFHS or FIH rulebook for sanctioned games.

The guide targets leaders who may have no prior field hockey experience, aligns with the **American Development Model (ADM)**, and includes photos, video pointers (FIH and **Leap Hockey**), and a sample **12-session** curriculum (~**60 minutes** each) in **PLAY–PRACTICE–PLAY** format. Core skills it highlights: **grip**, **stance**, **ball carrying**, **eliminations**, **passing**, **receiving**, and **tackling** (not American-football-style tackling). It covers starter equipment (including the **GO Ball** for beginners), notes that **shinguards** and **mouth guards** are standard in official play but often optional in intro PE when using softer balls, and points to **USA Field Hockey** membership, **In the Circle**, state chapters, and club finder.

The PDF excerpts **GAME ON Field Hockey** (**GO Field Hockey**): USA Field Hockey’s official **small-sided** intro format — **4v4**, **no goalkeepers**, short periods, **center pass** to start, **free hits** after fouls (**3 yards** of space), score when the ball crosses the **goal line**, and shared roles (warm-up leader, equipment, **umpire**). Full **GAME ON** docs and **GO Cards** live on USA Field Hockey’s site; **sportdevelopment@usafieldhockey.com** is listed for questions. The guide also points to **NFHSLearn.com** for NFHS field hockey **coaching**, **goalkeeping**, and **officiating** courses, plus FIH Academy and Positive Coaching Alliance resources.

## Core ideas

- **Circle (shooting circle)**: Goals generally require a **shot at goal** from inside the circle (including controlled deflections) — exact definitions of *goal*, *shot at goal*, and circle play sit in the current FIH text; always verify wording.
- **Fouls (examples)**: Dangerous raised balls, **third-party obstruction** (illegal screening), **stick interference**, playing the **rounded** side of the stick, **advancing** with feet or body, and careless **physical contact** are common things umpires manage. **“No feet”** for outfield players has nuances when contact is accidental and offers no advantage.
- **Free hit**: Awarded for many infractions, taken near the spot of the foul with a **stationary** ball. Restart may be a **self-pass** or a pass to a teammate; when it is not a self-pass, the ball usually must move about **1 m** before another attacker plays it, and opponents typically stay **5 m** back until then. An attacking foul in the circle often becomes a defending free hit along the **16-yard line** (exact lines and labels depend on the code).
- **Cards**: Green, yellow, and red address conduct and safety; **durations and procedures** follow the rulebook in force for that match (they are not identical across every competition).
- **Penalty corner (short corner)**: Structured restart from the end line after certain circle fouls or when defense deliberately sends the ball over the back line. Attack and defense take set positions until the insert; **how high the first shot** may cross the goal line depends on whether it is a **hit** versus a **controlled flick** — read the code in use.
- **Penalty stroke**: One attacker against the goalkeeper from the **penalty spot** (~7 yards) for certain deliberate fouls or fouls that deny a probable goal; the striker usually **pushes or flicks** after the whistle.
- **Substitutions**: **Unlimited rolling** substitution is common in modern formats, including NCAA play; timing and interchange zones follow the published regulations.

## Restarts at a glance

When the ball leaves over the **sideline**, the opponent usually takes a **side-in** where it went out (often with a quick self-pass or pass). When it leaves over the **back line**, who restarts and **where** depends on **who touched it last**, **intent**, and **which competition** is using which rulebook. Do not assume one global “long corner” spot — **NCAA** and **FIH** presentations can differ.

## Indoor, Hockey5s, and other formats

Indoor hockey uses **boards**, smaller teams, and different restart details. **Hockey5s**, **para hockey**, and **beach** formats have their own rule sets. The [USA Field Hockey Rules of Field Hockey](https://www.usafieldhockey.com/rules-of-hockey) hub branches to outdoor modifications, indoor, briefings, and additional formats.

## Where to read the real text

Use the **FIH Rules of Field Hockey** for international wording and the **USA Field Hockey** hub for US outdoor regulations, indoor links, **umpire signals** and **briefings**, and pointers to **NCAA** modifications. For **high school**, use the **NFHS** rulebook. For **introducing young players** in schools or community programs, use USA Field Hockey’s **FUNdamental Field Hockey Guide** and **GAME ON** materials as written by the national body — they do not replace the rulebook for sanctioned games. This page is only a map, not a substitute.
