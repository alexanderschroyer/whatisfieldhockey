## Women’s field hockey and the NCAA

**NCAA Division I** women’s field hockey is the main place Americans associate with large rosters, facilities, and **athletic scholarships** (within NCAA limits). **Division II** may offer partial athletic aid; **Division III** does not offer athletic scholarships but still offers **academic** and **need-based** aid. Always read each school’s financial-aid materials.

On the field, **college** games follow **NCAA rule modifications** on top of the international rulebook (for example some **restart** details). Current booklets are linked from [USA Field Hockey — Rules of Field Hockey](https://www.usafieldhockey.com/rules-of-hockey).

## NAIA and other routes

Some **NAIA** institutions sponsor field hockey; aid rules differ from the NCAA. Junior college pathways exist in places but are less central than in some other sports — check current catalogs.

## Recruiting reality

- **Film and events**: Club tournaments, regional training centers, and ID camps matter; requirements change by school and level.
- **Academics**: Eligibility and admissions are separate tracks; a scholarship offer still requires clearing the registrar’s bar.
- **Timing**: Contact rules and calendars update; the NCAA publishes recruiting guides by sport.

## This page is not compliance advice

Coaches, families, and international students face different rules. **Nothing here replaces** the NCAA Eligibility Center, a school’s compliance office, or a qualified advisor.

## Why emphasize women

In the US, **women’s** field hockey most often aligns with **varsity scholarship structures** people expect from “college sports.” Men’s players may pursue excellent college experiences through club or other sports, but the same **NCAA field hockey scholarship** pathway is not mirrored for men. Describe that honestly when you grow the game.
